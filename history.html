History of Japanese Curry

Japanese curry, known as "kare", is a popular dish in Japan with a rich history that reflects the nation's interactions with other cultures.
Here is a brief overview of its history:

Early Introduction
19th Century: Curry was introduced to Japan during the Meiji era (1868-1912) by the British, who had adapted Indian curry to suit their tastes during their colonial rule in India. The British brought curry powder and recipes to Japan, where it was first served in the Japanese Navy to prevent beriberi, a disease caused by a vitamin B1 deficiency.
Adaptation and Popularization
Late 19th to Early 20th Century: Curry began to spread beyond the military and was adapted to Japanese tastes, resulting in a milder and thicker version compared to Indian curry. This adaptation often included ingredients like potatoes, carrots, and onions, along with meat, typically pork, beef, or chicken.
Post-War Expansion
Post-World War II: Curry became increasingly popular as it was inexpensive and easy to prepare. The introduction of ready-made curry roux in block form in the 1950s by companies like S&B Foods significantly contributed to its widespread popular. The roux made it convenient for Japanese households to prepare curry at home.
Cultural Integration
Mid to Late 20th Century: Japanese curry became a staple of Japanese cuisine, often served with rice (kareraisu), udon noodles (kare udon), or even as a filling for bread (kare pan). School cafeterias began serving it regularly, and it became a comfort food for many Japanese people.
Contemporary Variations
21st Century: Today, Japanese curry is enjoyed in various forms, including regional specialties like Kanazawa curry, which is known for its rich, thick sauce and is often served with tonkatsu. There are also many curry restaurants and chains, such as Coco Ichibanya, which offers a wide range of curry dishes with customizable spice levels and toppings.
Global Influence
Japanese curry has also gained popularity outside of Japan, particularly in countries with significant Japanese expatriate communities. It has influenced and been influenced by various culinary traditions around the world, contributing to the global appreciation of this unique dish.
